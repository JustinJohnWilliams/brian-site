  • 10 Sept 2012: The link to Tribble's assessment of and response to Dijkstra's Considered Harmful is posted below (part II can be skipped). As noted in class, students should read this paper, especially with the objective of obtaining their own understanding of Dijkstra's paper. Students should write brief (approximately 1 paragraph apiece) answers to the following questions (Due Monday, 17 Sept 2012, 3pt quiz grade):
    1. Is Tribble's argument consistent with Dijkstra's?
      • In particular, does it follow / fit the framework that Dijkstra presents?
    2. Is Tribble's argument convincing?
      • Do you believe that his argument should be given merit by someone who believes that all gotos are bad?
      • Can you provide counter-arguments?
      • In other words, it is not enough to simply agree with him - you must objectively assess the argument he is making.

Readings

Students are expected to read and understand the articles listed below, or to come to class with questions that would aid their understanding. These papers may not be explicitly re-stated during class; instead, their purpose is to provide points of connections between the different threads of the class (and the authors have generally said it better anyways).

  • Dijkstra's A Case against the GO TO Statement (aka GO TO Considered Harmful)
  • Go To Considered Harmful: A Retrospective by David R. Tribble. Elaborates on Dijkstra's paper above, then further argues that not all gotos are harmful.
    • Part II (describing how control statements are translated to assembly) can be skipped.
  • Notes on the M4 Macro Language: a tutorial on m4 by Michael Breen
